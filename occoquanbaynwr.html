Occoquan Bay NWR - April Week 3

Warblers
Prairie Warbler | Setophaga discolor

Notes:

  • Long tail is often pumped up and down
  • Reddish streaks on back are distinctive, but can be difficult to see or absent on immatures

Size:
Yellow Warbler | Setophaga petechia

Notes:

  • Some immatures can be almost completely gray
  • Rather stout bill for a warbler
  • Favors brushy habitats near water, often foraging in shrubs fairly low to the ground

Size:
Palm Warbler | Setophaga palmarum

Notes:

  • Known for constantly bobbing its tail
  • Often forages on the ground, unlike most other warblers

Size:
Yellow-rumped Warbler | Setophaga coronata

Notes:

  • “Myrtle” breeds from the eastern U.S.
  • White throat that wraps around below the cheek
  • Often sallies out from a conspicuous perch to snatch insects

Size:
Yellow-throated Warbler | Setophaga dominica

Notes:

  • Slender, long-billed
  • Forages like a nuthatch, often creeping along branches and hanging upside-down

Size:
Black-throated Green Warbler | Setophaga virens

Notes:

  • Only breeding males have solid black throat
  • Look for dark streaks on white flanks and yellow wash on vent

Size:
Northern Parula | Setophaga americana

Notes:

  • Short-tailed warbler with a sharply pointed, bicolored bill

Size:
American Redstart | Setophaga ruticilla

Notes:

  • Habitually flicks and fans tail while foraging for insects
  • Song is extremely variable

Size:
Common Yellowthroat | Geothlypis trichas

Notes:

  • Often hides in thick vegetation, but males often perch conspicuously when singing
  • Found in shrubby wet areas, including marshes, forest edges, and fields

Size:
Prothonotary Warbler | Protonotaria citrea

Notes:

  • Typically stays in the understory, but males can perch up high when singing

Size:
Worm-eating Warbler | Helmitheros vermivorum

Notes:

  • Note black and tan stripes on head, long, sharp bill, pinkish legs.
  • Skulky, often seen in the low understory, or on ground probing clumps of dead leaves

Size:
Louisiana Waterthrush | Parkesia motacilla

Notes:

  • Stays on or near the ground, constantly bobbing its rear end up and down
  • Males sometime perch up high to sing
  • Favors fast-moving streams with lots of rocks to hop on
  • Best distinguished by song: several piercing clear whistles followed by a complex jumble of notes

Size:
Northern Waterthrush | Parkesia noveboracensis

Notes:

  • Stays on or near the ground, constantly bobbing its rear end up and down
  • Very similar to Louisiana Waterthrush, but usually more yellowish base color below, duller legs, and narrower off-white eyebrow
  • Also look for small streaks on the throat (clean white on Louisiana)
  • Best distinguished by song: a loud, two- or three-parted descending series of chips.

Size:
Sparrows
Song Sparrow | Melospiza melodia

Notes:

  • Fairly large with a long, rounded tail
  • Look especially for broad dark mustache stripe
  • Larger, longer-tailed and usually more rusty than Savannah Sparrow

Size:
Swamp Sparrow | Melospiza georgiana

Notes:

  • Slightly smaller than Song Sparrow, with a blurrier look overall
  • Breeds in cattail marshes and other wetlands

Size:
White-throated Sparrow | Zonotrichia albicollis

Notes:

  • Large, long-tailed sparrow
  • Two morphs with different head colors: white-striped and tan-striped
  • Note the grayish bill, unlike White-crowned Sparrow

Size:
Savannah Sparrow | Passerculus sandwichensis

Notes:

  • Medium-sized, short-tailed sparrow
  • Distinctive yellow patch in front of eye can be indistinct or absent
  • Almost always found in open areas
  • Tends to be found in more open habitats than Song Sparrow; also smaller and shorter-tailed

Size:
Field Sparrow | Spizella pusilla

Notes:

  • Small, slender, blank-faced sparrow
  • Always note small pink bill, plain face, and thin white eyering

Size:
Chipping Sparrow | Spizella passerina

Notes:

  • Small, slender
  • Plain grayish breast, rusty cap, and black eyeline in breeding season

Size:
House Sparrow | Passer domesticus

Notes:

  • Males have smart black bibs, bright rufous napes, and stunningly patterned wings

Size:
Icterids
Brown-headed Cowbird | Molothrus ater

Notes:

  • Stout bill, short tail and stocky body
  • Forages by walking on the ground

Size:
Orchard Oriole | Icterus spurius

Notes:

  • Small, slender oriole
  • Breeds in open woodlands, river edges, pastures with scattered trees

Size:
Yellow-breasted Chat | Icteria virens

Notes:

  • Strange, long-tailed tanager-like bird with thick bill
  • Known for its skulking habits; often difficult to see in dense thickets, shrubby areas, and field edges
  • In breeding season, males can sit on conspicuous perches to sing and even perform a flight display

Size:
Vireos, Kinglets, Flycatchers
White-eyed Vireo | Vireo griseus

Notes:

  • Small, active vireo of dense shrubbery
  • Distinctive yellow spectacles contrast with gray head
  • Also look for two white wingbars, green back, and yellow sides
  • Occurs in dense second growth, forest edge, and thickets; more often heard than seen; scratchy "chick-burio-chick" song

Size:
Red-eyed Vireo | Vireo olivaceus

Notes:

  • Larger and thicker-billed than warblers
  • Often sings nearly nonstop throughout the day; listen for repetitive musical phrases coming from the treetops

Size:
Ruby-crowned Kinglet | Corthylio calendula

Notes:

  • Tiny, hyperactive songbird with an uneven white eyering
  • Often forages quite low to the ground
  • Energetic, moving quickly and flicking its wings

Size:
Golden-crowned Kinglet | Regulus satrapa

Notes:

  • Tiny, hyperactive songbird usually found in conifer trees
  • Look for black stripes on the head
  • Breeds in coniferous forests, usually staying high in trees
  • Very active; often hovers and flicks wings as it forages

Size:
Eastern Kingbird | Tyrannus tyrannus

Notes:

  • Fairly large flycatcher; Look for white tail tip
  • Fairly common and conspicuous; perches out in the open, often on tall trees, snags, fences, and utility lines
  • Watches for large insects and makes quick flights to snatch them

Size:
Eastern Phoebe | Sayornis phoebe

Notes:

  • Watch for constant tail-wagging
  • Usually seen singly or in pairs in relatively open habitats such as woodland edge, brushy fields, or edges of ponds
  • Listen for its harsh “FEE-bee” song in the spring, but don’t get confused by Black-capped Chickadee’s higher-pitched song of a similar pattern

Size:
Great Crested Flycatcher | Myiarchus crinitus

Notes:

  • Fairly large flycatcher
  • Most easily identified by voice: listen for loud, rising “queeEEEP” and various rolling, burry calls

Size:
Swallows
Tree Swallow | Tachycineta bicolor

Notes:

  • A medium-sized swallow, with gleaming white underparts
  • Always note compact shape, fairly broad wings, and slightly notched tail
  • Listen for cheery gurgling calls

Size:
Purple Martin | Progne subis

Notes:

  • Large, dark swallow
  • Gives a distinctive liquid gurgling call
  • During migration and winter, identification is complicated by several other extremely similar species

Size:
Northern Rough-winged Swallow | Stelgidopteryx serripennis

Notes:

  • Plain brown above with indistinct brown wash across throat and breast
  • Often seen near water, sometimes in mixed flocks
  • Compare with Bank Swallow, which has a contrasting dark chest band

Size:
Finches, Wrens, Thrushes
American Goldfinch | Spinus tristis

Notes:

  • Sharply pointed bill is pink in summer, grayish-brown in winter.
  • Small head, long wings, and short, notched tail
  • Active and acrobatic

Size:
Indigo Bunting | Passerina cyanea

Notes:

  • Males often sing from a high exposed perch

Size:
Winter Wren | Troglodytes hiemalis

Notes:

  • Tiny, short-tailed, plain-brown
  • Subtly patterned, with indistinct pale eyebrow
  • Constantly keeps tail cocked at an angle
  • Prefers wetter areas with thick tangles, often around treefalls and moss
  • Darker, smaller, and shorter-tailed than House Wren

Size:
Northern House Wren | Troglodytes aedon

Notes:

  • Small brownish wren, usually rather plain overall, lacks white eyebrow
  • Larger, longer tailed, and generally slightly paler than Winter Wren
  • Often perches conspicuously when singing, but otherwise fairly secretive

Size:
Hermit Thrush | Catharus guttatus

Notes:

  • Medium brown above with contrasting reddish tail.
  • Dark spots on breast do not continue all the way down belly like Wood Thrush
  • Found in wooded areas; often hops on the ground or skulks in dense patches of shrubbery

Size:
Wood Thrush | Hylocichla mustelina

Notes:

  • Smaller than a robin, but larger than Hermit Thrush
  • Sticks to wooded areas, often hopping on the ground
  • More often heard than seen: listen for its incredible, flute-like song

Size:
Cuckoos
Yellow-billed Cuckoo | Coccyzus americanus

Notes:

  • Slender, long-tailed bird that is more often heard than seen
  • Wings flash rufous, especially obvious in flight
  • Stealthy and shy in dense forests and riparian areas, often sitting motionless for long periods of time

Size:
Ducks
Ruddy Duck | Oxyura jamaicensis

Notes:

  • Small, compact duck with a long, fan-shaped tail, often held sticking up out of water
  • Often in tight groups bobbing like corks on ponds and bays
  • Dives to forage on aquatic invertebrates; not often seen flying.

Size:
Bufflehead | Bucephala albeola

Notes:

  • Small, compact diving duck with large head and small gray bill
  • Much smaller than Common Goldeneye with which it often associates

Size:
Mallard | Anas platyrhynchos

Notes:

  • Females can be tricky to distinguish from American Black Duck, which is darker

Size:
Blue-winged Teal | Spatula discors

Notes:

  • Small duck with chalky-blue patches on the upperwing (visible in flight)
  • Bold white crescent in front of eye and polka-dotted sides
  • Usually found in shallow wetlands or marshes, where it can occur in pairs or flocks, sometimes mixed with other species of ducks
  • Female Green-winged Teal is more compact, darker, and has a different wing pattern in flight

Size:
Ring-necked Duck | Aythya collaris

Notes:

  • Relatively small diving duck with a tall peaked crown
  • Both sexes have a white band near the tip of gray bill

Size:
Lesser Scaup | Aythya affinis

Notes:

  • Peak-headed diving duck
  • Females are brown overall with white patch at base of bill and yellow eye
  • Very similar to Greater Scaup; note Lesser’s peaked head shape with angle near the rear of crown, slimmer cheeks, less extensive white stripe on wing in flight, and smaller, narrower bill
  • Also similar to Ring-necked Duck, but Lesser Scaup has grayer back and whiter sides

Size:
Red-breasted Merganser | Mergus serrator

Notes:

  • Long-bodied duck with a thin bill and shaggy crest
  • Separated from Common Merganser by thinner bill, shaggier crest, and slimmer shape overall
  • Dives to catch fish on large bodies of water,

Size:
Gulls, Terns
Caspian Tern | Hydroprogne caspia

Notes:

  • Largest tern in the world
  • Thick, bright-red bill is distinctive
  • Smooth wingbeats, more gull-like than choppy flight of small-bodied terns

Size:
Forster's Tern | Sterna forsteri

Notes:

  • Smaller than all the gulls, with thinner and more pointed bill
  • Distinguished from other terns by pale whitish wings and orange-based bill in summer, and isolated black ear patch in winter
  • Forages by circling and hovering over bays, ponds, and lakes, then dropping to snatch fish
  • Snappy, quick wingbeats and acrobatic in flight

Size:
Ring-billed Gull | Larus delawarensis

Notes:

  • Fairly small gull, bold black ring around the bill
  • Note pale eye and yellow legs
  • Immatures are mottled brownish overall; note pink bill with black tip

Size:
Laughing Gull | Leucophaeus atricilla

Notes:

  • Slender, medium-sized gull with long wings
  • Juveniles are browner with neat scaly pattern on upperparts

Size:
Shorebirds, Herons
Greater Yellowlegs | Tringa melanoleuca

Notes:

  • Fairly large shorebird with bright yellow legs
  • Greater is larger overall with longer, thicker, more upturned bill, longer neck, blockier head, and bigger chest
  • Forages actively on mudflats and in shallow pools and marshes, often in loose mixed flocks with Lesser Yellowlegs

Size:
Spotted Sandpiper | Actitis macularius

Notes:

  • Constantly bobs its tail while working edges of streams, ponds, and lakes
  • Several individuals may be found at the same body of water, but never forms tight flocks
  • Distinctive wingbeats: snappy and below horizontal

Size:
American Woodcock | Scolopax minor

Notes:

  • Favors wooded or shrubby areas, usually near open fields, always on the ground
  • Might be confused with Wilson’s Snipe, but woodcock is not nearly as dark and patterned
  • Secretive and rarely seen well in daytime

Size:
King Rail | Rallus elegans

Notes:

  • Very similar to Clapper Rail, especially on the Gulf Coast
  • Habitat is a good first clue: King prefers freshwater marshes with extensive cattails or other reeds; rarely found in brackish or saltwater marshes like Clapper
  • King is also brighter overall with more contrasting pattern on the sides and upperparts

Size:
Little Blue Heron | Egretta caerulea

Notes:

  • Adult is distinctive: mostly grayish-blue with a contrasting deep purplish head and neck
  • Also note greenish legs and bicolored bill
  • Take care to separate from Snowy Egret; note Little Blue always has dull greenish legs, grayish or greenish lores, and usually a two-toned bill
  • Little Blue also forages more slowly, often pausing for long periods of time with neck outstretched and bill pointed down
